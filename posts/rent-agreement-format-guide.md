### The Legal Secret: Why Exactly "11 Months" in India?

Have you ever wondered why almost every residential rent agreement in India is drafted for exactly 11 months? It sounds like a strange random number, but it is actually a very clever legal workaround used by landlords and lawyers to bypass two strict Indian laws.

#### 1. Bypassing the Registration Act, 1908
According to Section 17 of the Indian Registration Act, 1908, any lease or tenancy agreement for a term exceeding one year (12 months or more) **must be mandatorily registered** at the local Sub-Registrar’s office. 

Registering a document involves paying heavy stamp duty charges and registration fees (which can range from 1% to 5% of the total annual rent depending on the state), plus making physical rounds of government offices. By keeping the contract length at exactly 11 months, the agreement falls out of the mandatory registration bracket, saving both time and money.

#### 2. Dodging the Strict Rent Control Acts
Each state in India has its own Rent Control Act (e.g., the Maharashtra Rent Control Act or the Delhi Rent Control Act). These older laws were heavily biased towards protecting tenants. Under these acts, if an agreement is registered and long-term, it becomes incredibly difficult for a landlord to evict a problematic tenant or increase the rent, even if the tenant stops paying. 

However, an 11-month contract is legally treated as a short-term "Leave and License" agreement, not a traditional lease. This means the strict, rigid rules of the Rent Control Act do not apply, giving the property owner peace of mind and the right to evict the tenant easily once the contract expires.

### 5 Critical Clauses You Must Verify Before Signing

When a broker hands you a drafted rent agreement, do not just blindly flip to the last page and sign it. Take your time, read it line-by-line, and ensure these five crucial lifesavers are included.

#### 1. The Security Deposit Refund Clause
This is where 90% of disputes happen. The agreement must explicitly state the exact amount of security deposit paid and, more importantly, the conditions for its refund. It should read that the deposit must be refunded *on the exact day* the tenant hands over the keys, after deducting valid outstanding electricity bills or actual damage repairs. Beware of vague phrases like "deductions for regular wear and tear"—painting charges should not be deducted from the tenant's pocket unless explicitly agreed upon.

#### 2. Notice Period and Lock-in Period
A "Lock-in Period" means neither the landlord can evict the tenant, nor the tenant can leave the house for a set period (usually 3 to 6 months). If the tenant leaves during this time, they forfeit their deposit. Ensure the agreement clearly defines the lock-in duration and the subsequent notice period (typically 1 or 2 months) required by either party to terminate the contract gracefully.

#### 3. Rent Escalation / Increase Percentage
If you plan to stay longer than 11 months, how much will the rent increase when the contract is renewed? In India, the standard practice is a **5% to 10% hike** on the existing monthly rent after the completion of 11 months. Having this percentage pre-written prevents the landlord from demanding an unfair double digit hike next year.

#### 4. Maintenance and Society Charges
Who pays the society's monthly maintenance fee? Who pays for the sudden breakdown of the water pump or lift repairs? Usually, the tenant pays for routine utility bills (electricity, water, internet) and minor operational repairs, while the landlord covers heavy society maintenance maintenance charges, structural repairs, and property taxes. Make sure this line is written clearly.

#### 5. Eviction Grounds
There should be clear conditions under which a landlord can ask the tenant to leave immediately—such as non-payment of monthly rent for two consecutive months, illegal commercial use of a residential property, or causing severe structural damage or nuisance to neighbors.

### Stamp Paper Value: How Much Do You Need?

A rent agreement written on a blank white piece of paper holds almost zero value in an Indian court. To make it a valid legal document, it must be executed on a **Non-Judicial Stamp Paper** or through an **e-Stamp Paper** (available in states like Karnataka, Maharashtra, Delhi, and Uttar Pradesh).

The required value of the stamp paper depends entirely on the state where the property is located:
* In states like **Delhi, Uttar Pradesh, and Haryana**, a **₹50 or ₹100** stamp paper is standard for an 11-month residential agreement.
* In states like **Karnataka and Maharashtra**, the stamp paper value is calculated dynamically based on the monthly rent and security deposit amount (usually around **₹200 or ₹500**).

**Is Notarisation Necessary?**
While an 11-month agreement doesn't need mandatory sub-registrar registration, getting it stamped and verified by a **Notary Public** is highly recommended. A notary stamp serves as proof that both the landlord and tenant signed the document willingly in front of an authorized legal officer, preventing either party from later claiming that their signature was forged.
