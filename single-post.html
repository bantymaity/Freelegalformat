How to File a Police Complaint in India: Complete Step-by-Step Guide (2025)

Whether you've been robbed, cheated, harassed, or are the victim of any crime — this guide explains exactly how to file a police complaint, what your rights are, and what to do if the police refuse to help.

💡 Quick Summary

A police complaint in India can be filed as an FIR (First Information Report) for cognizable offences (serious crimes) or a General Diary (GD) entry for non-cognizable offences. You have the right to file it at any police station, and the police are legally bound to register it. This guide walks you through the entire process step by step.

1. FIR vs. GD: What's the Difference?

Before you go to the police station, it's important to know which type of complaint applies to your situation.

First Information Report (FIR)

An FIR is filed for cognizable offences — these are serious crimes where the police can arrest someone without a warrant. Examples include theft, robbery, assault, rape, murder, dowry harassment, cheating above a certain amount, and cyber fraud.

Under Section 154 of the Code of Criminal Procedure (CrPC), the police are legally required to register an FIR if you report a cognizable offence. They cannot refuse.

General Diary (GD) Entry

A GD entry is for non-cognizable offences — less serious incidents where the police cannot arrest without a warrant and need a magistrate's order to investigate. Examples include public nuisance, minor quarrels, and lost item reporting.

⚠️ Important

If you are unsure whether your case is cognizable or non-cognizable, always ask for an FIR. The police will advise you if a GD is more appropriate. Never settle for a verbal acknowledgement alone.

2. Who Can File a Police Complaint?

  • Any citizen of India can file a complaint, regardless of age, gender, or religion.
  • The victim of the crime can file it personally.
  • A witness who has seen the crime can also file a complaint on behalf of the victim.
  • In the case of minors or persons with disability, a parent, guardian, or trusted adult can file on their behalf.
  • You do not need a lawyer to file a police complaint.

3. What Should Your Complaint Letter Contain?

A well-written complaint letter makes it easier for the police to register your FIR quickly. It should include:

  1. Your full name, address, and contact number
  2. Date, time, and exact location of the incident
  3. Clear description of what happened — in chronological order
  4. Name or description of the accused (if known)
  5. Names of any witnesses (if available)
  6. List of any losses — items stolen, injuries caused, financial loss
  7. Evidence you have — screenshots, photographs, transaction IDs, etc.
  8. Your signature at the end of the letter

4. Step-by-Step: How to File Your Complaint

Step 1: Prepare Your Documents

Before visiting the police station, gather the following:

  • Your written complaint letter (use our free generator to draft it)
  • A photocopy of your Aadhaar card or other ID proof
  • Any evidence — photos, videos, transaction records, screenshots
  • List of witness names and contact details (if any)

Step 2: Visit the Correct Police Station

Go to the police station in whose jurisdiction (area) the crime happened. If the crime happened online (cyber fraud), you can go to your nearest police station or file on the Cyber Crime Portal (cybercrime.gov.in).

If you don't know the right jurisdiction, any police station is obligated to record your complaint and forward it to the correct one — this is called Zero FIR.

💡 Zero FIR

A Zero FIR can be filed at ANY police station, even outside the jurisdiction where the crime occurred. The police must register it and transfer it to the correct station. This was made mandatory by the Supreme Court of India.

Step 3: Meet the SHO or Duty Officer

At the police station, ask to meet the Station House Officer (SHO) or the duty officer. Hand them your written complaint. They will record the information and — if it is a cognizable offence — are legally required to register your FIR.

Step 4: Get Your FIR Copy

Once the FIR is registered, the police must give you a free copy of the FIR. This is your right under Section 154(2) of the CrPC. Keep this copy safely — you will need it for follow-ups, courts, insurance claims, and more.

The FIR copy will have a unique FIR number. Use this number to track the status of your case.

Step 5: Follow Up Regularly

After the FIR is registered, the police have a duty to investigate. Follow up every 7–10 days. If there is no action within 60 days, you can approach the Magistrate's Court for orders directing the police to act.

5. What If the Police Refuse to Register Your FIR?

Unfortunately, this happens. If the police refuse to register your FIR, you have several options:

  1. Meet the Superintendent of Police (SP) — You can send your complaint by post or email to the SP of the district. Under Section 154(3) CrPC, the SP can direct the police station to register the FIR.
  2. Approach the Magistrate — File a complaint directly with the Judicial Magistrate under Section 156(3) CrPC. The Magistrate can direct the police to register and investigate your case.
  3. File an Online Complaint — Many states have online portals for police complaints (e.g., UP Police, Delhi Police). You can also file on the National Cyber Crime Reporting Portal.
  4. File a Writ Petition — In extreme cases, approach the High Court for a writ directing the police to act. A lawyer is helpful for this step.
⚠️ Important Right

Refusing to register an FIR for a cognizable offence is a criminal offence by the police officer under Section 166A of the Indian Penal Code (now BNS). You can file a complaint against the officer with the Police Complaints Authority or the Superintendent.

6. Alternatives: Filing a Complaint Online

You can now file police complaints online through the following channels:

  • Cyber Crime Portal: cybercrime.gov.in — for online fraud, financial cyber crime, social media harassment
  • State Police Websites: Most states have online portals (e.g., Delhi Police, UP Police 112)
  • National Emergency: 112 — For immediate response, call 112 from anywhere in India
  • Women's Helpline: 1091 — For complaints related to crimes against women

7. Practical Tips for a Stronger Complaint

  • Be specific: Mention exact dates, times, and locations. Vague complaints are harder to act on.
  • Keep evidence safe: Screenshot all online conversations, save transaction IDs, photograph injuries or damage immediately.
  • Take a witness: If possible, take a family member or trusted person when you go to the police station.
  • Write in simple language: Don't use complex words. Write as if you're telling a friend what happened.
  • Make copies: Always keep a copy of everything you submit.
  • Stay calm and firm: The police must help you. Know your rights.

8. Conclusion

Filing a police complaint is your fundamental right as an Indian citizen. The law is on your side — the police are legally required to register your FIR for cognizable offences, and you have multiple escalation options if they refuse.
